Differential drive car

Pranav A. Bhounsule

1 Differential drive car

Fig.~\ref{fig:car} (a) shows the differential drive car. There are two wheels, each powered by a motor. There is a castor wheel in the front (not shown). A castor allows the robot to do quick turns. The radius of each wheel is $r$ and the distance between the two wheels is $2b$. The speed of the left wheel is $\dot{\phi}_l$ and that of the right wheel is $\dot{\phi}_r$, both of which are controlled.

2 Forward Kinematics

Top view of differential drive car (a) The world or fixed frame $X_0-Y_0$ is shown to coincide with robot local frame. (b) The local frame $X_1-Y_1$ is attached to the robot and moves with it. The steer or heading angles is $\theta$ and the point $C$ is midway between two wheels. It has the coordinates ${x^1_c, y^1_c}$ in the local frame and ${x^0_c, y^0_c}$ in the world or fixed frame. Here we find the differential equations for the position of $C$, ${\dot{x}^0_c, \dot{y}^0_c}$ and heading $\dot{\theta}$.

   The forward kinematics problem is to find the position of the point C at time some time $t$, $(x^0_c(t),y^0_c(t))$. Here we will write an expression for the rate in frame 1, $(\dot{x}^1_c(t),\dot{y}^1_c(t))$ then find the rate in frame 0, $(\dot{x}^0_c(t),\dot{y}^0_c(t))$. We will integrate this equation with the given initial condition $(x^0_c(t=0),y^0_c(t=0))$ and control history (speed of both wheels, $\dot{\phi}_l$ and $\dot{\phi}_r$) to find the position $(x^0_c(t),y^0_c(t))$
   There are some differences between the manipulator and the differential drive robot. In the manipulator we were able to write equations for the degrees of freedom, $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$. However, we cannot do so for the differential drive robot. We can only write the equation for the rates; the rate of change of position of the mid-point C between the two wheels, $\dot{x}_c$, $\dot{y}_c$, and the rate of change of heading angle $\dot{\theta}$.
   We use the Fig.~\ref{fig:car} to derive an expression for the kinematics of the robot for the heading motion. We use two frames, $O_0-X_0-Y_0$ is the fixed or world frame and $O_1-X_1-Y_1$ is the local frame attached to the car and that moves with the car.
   We derive the velocity of C in the frame $O_1-X_1-Y_1$. If the left wheel is stationary and the right moves with a speed $\dot{\phi}_r$, then the $\dot{x}^1_c = 0.5 r \dot{\phi}_r$. The factor $0.5$ is due to point C being mid-way between the wheels. One can similarly argue that if right wheel is stationary and the left moves with a speed $\dot{\phi}_l$, then the $\dot{x}^1_c = 0.5 r \dot{\phi}_l$. However, if both wheels spin the speed would be \begin{align} \dot{x}^1_c &= 0.5 r (\dot{\phi}_r + \dot{\phi}_l) \nonumber \end{align}    Since the wheels can only move the point C in the forward direction (along $X_1$), the y-component of the velocity (along $Y_1$) is going to be zero. Thus \begin{align} \dot{y}^1_c &= 0 \nonumber \end{align}    The x- and y- components of the velocity in frame $O_0-X_0-Y_0$ can be rewritten using the rotation matrix as \begin{align} \dot{c}^0 &= R^0_1 \dot{c}^1 \nonumber \\ \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^0_c \\ \dot{y}^0_c \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^1_c \\ \dot{y}^1_c \end{bmatrix} \nonumber \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 r (\dot{\phi}_r + \dot{\phi}_l) \nonumber \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{align} The above expressions are simplified to \begin{align} \dot{x}_c^0 &= 0.5 r (\dot{\phi}_r + \dot{\phi}_l) \cos\theta \nonumber \\ \dot{y}_c^0 &= 0.5 r (\dot{\phi}_r + \dot{\phi}_l) \sin\theta \nonumber \end{align}    Next, we evaluate the rate of change of heading $\dot{\theta}$. If the left wheel is stationary and the right wheel moves with a non-zero speed $\dot{\phi}_r$, then the car will start turning counter-clockwise pivoting about the stationary left wheel with a turning radius of $2b$. The arc length moved by the right wheel is $r \dot{\phi}_r$ is also equal to $2b \dot{\theta}$. Thus $2b \dot{\theta} = r \phi_r \rightarrow \dot{\theta} = \frac {r \phi_r}{2b}$. Similarly, if the right wheel is stationary and the left wheel is turning, the resulting rate of change of heading is $\dot{\theta} = -\frac {r \phi_l}{2b}$. If both wheels are spinning then \begin{align} \dot{\theta} &= 0.5 \frac{r}{b} (\dot{\phi}_r - \dot{\phi}_l) \nonumber \end{align} To simplify analysis, we substitute $v = 0.5 r (\dot{\phi}_r + \dot{\phi}_l)$ which is the forward speed and $\omega = \frac{0.5 r}{b} (\dot{\phi}_r - \dot{\phi}_l)$ which is the angular speed of the car. Thus, the complete set of equations are \begin{align} \dot{x}_c^0 &= v \cos\theta \label{eqn:rot1} \\ \dot{y}_c^0 &= v \sin\theta \label{eqn:rot2} \\ \dot{\theta} &= \omega \label{eqn:rot3} \end{align}    Unlike the manipulator, the forward kinematics of the differential drive car is a differential equation. In order to animate the system, we will need to find the position $x_c^0(t_i)$, $y_c^0(t_i)$, and $\theta(t_i)$ where $t_i$ is the time step $i=0, 1, 2,...$. We do this by integrating the equations of motion using Euler's method. Assume that $h$ is the time step and is given, the known initial states of $x_c^0(t_0)$, $y_c^0(t_0)$, and $\theta(t_0)$. Then we can find the position and heading using the formula below. \begin{align} x_c^0(t_{i+1}) &= x_c^0(t_i) + v(t_i) \cos\theta(t_i) h \nonumber\\ y_c^0(t_{i+1}) &= y_c^0(t_i) + v(t_i) \sin\theta(t_i) h \nonumber \\ \theta(t_{i+1}) &= \theta(t_{i}) + \omega(t_i) h \nonumber \end{align} Note that the input to the system is the speed $v(t_i)$ and heading direction $\omega(t_i)$. Open and run the MATLAB file diff_drive.m.

3 Inverse Kinetics

Inverse kinematics for the differential drive car: The goal is to get a point P at a distance of $p_x$ and $p_y$ from $O_1$ and along the local frame $O_1-X_1-Y_1$ to trace the reference trajectory given by $(x_{{\scriptsize ref}}(t),y_{{\scriptsize ref}}(t))$.}

From Fig.~\ref{fig:car2} we can write \begin{align} p^0 &= R^0_1 p^1 \nonumber \\ c^0 &= R^0_1 c^1 \nonumber \end{align} Thus, \begin{align} p^0 - c^0 &= R^0_1 (p^1-c^1) \nonumber \\ \begin{bmatrix} x^0_p - x^0_c \\ y^0_p - y^0_c \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x^1_p - x^1_c \\ y^1_p - y^1_c \end{bmatrix} \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \end{bmatrix} \end{align} Rewriting \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} x^0_p \\ y^0_p \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} x^0_c \\ y^0_c \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \end{bmatrix} \end{align} We can also write the coordinates of C given point P (this will be needed in simulation) \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} x^0_c \\ y^0_c \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} x^0_p \\ y^0_p \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \end{bmatrix} \end{align} Differentiating with respect to time \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^0_p \\ \dot{y}^0_p \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^0_c \\ \dot{y}^0_c \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -\dot{\theta}\sin\theta & -\dot{\theta}\cos\theta \\ \dot{\theta}\cos\theta & -\dot{\theta}\sin\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \end{bmatrix} \end{align} Substituting $\dot{x}^0_c = v \cos\theta$, $\dot{y}^0_c = v \sin\theta$, and $\dot{\theta} = \omega$. \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^0_p \\ \dot{y}^0_p \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} v \cos\theta \\ v \sin\theta \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} (-p_x \sin\theta - p_y \cos\theta) \omega \\ (p_x \cos\theta - p_y \sin\theta ) \omega \end{bmatrix} \end{align} Rewriting \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x}^0_p \\ \dot{y}^0_p \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & (-p_x \sin\theta - p_y \cos\theta) \\ \sin\theta & (p_x \cos\theta - p_y \sin\theta ) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v \\ \omega \end{bmatrix} \end{align} Let $x_p$ and $y_p$ be the current x- and y-position of point and let $x_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}$ and $y_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}$ be the reference position. We set $\dot{x}^0_p = K_{px} (x_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}-x^0_p)$ and $\dot{y}^0_p = K_{py} (y_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}-y^0_p)$ where $K_{px}$ and $K_{py}$ are user chosen gains. Substituting in the above equation and solving for $v$ and $\omega$ we get \begin{align} \begin{bmatrix} v \\ \omega \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta - \frac{p_y}{p_x}\sin\theta & \sin\theta + \frac{p_y}{p_x} \cos\theta \\ -\frac{1}{p_x} \sin\theta & \frac{1}{p_x} \cos\theta \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} K_{px} (x_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}-x^0_p) \\ K_{py} (y_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}-y^0_p) \end{bmatrix} \label{eqn:controls} \end{align} Open and run the MATLAB file {\it diff\_drive\_ik.m}. This file shows how to draw the astroid given by $x_{\mbox{_ref}}(t) = \cos^3(\frac{\pi t}{5})$ and $y_{\mbox{\scriptsize ref}}(t) = \sin^3(\frac{\pi t}{5})$. This is how the simulation is done. We find the controls, $v$ and $\omega$ from Eqn.~\ref{eqn:controls} and use that to integrate the Eqns~\ref{eqn:rot1}, \ref{eqn:rot2}, and \ref{eqn:rot3}.
